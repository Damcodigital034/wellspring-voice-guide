The Anchors

When in doubt, come back to these

Three real examples from your own practice that define exactly how Wellspring sounds. Read these first, every time.

01
“Hello there! Life can be a real whirlwind, right? That’s where I come in — to be a safe haven just for you.”

“Your story is incredibly important, and I am excited to learn about you.”
Melissa’s bio
A conversational opener. A small rhetorical question that invites rather than pitches. “Just for you” is small but human. Closes with warmth, not a sale.
02
“In today’s fast-paced world, it’s all too easy to feel overwhelmed, uncertain, and disconnected.”

“This journey won’t be about avoiding challenges — it will be about embracing them.”
Tabitha’s bio
Names the feeling concretely without diagnosing it. The “won’t be X, will be Y” reframe gives the reader something real to hold onto.
03
“We are here for you.”

“Feeling better starts with feeling connected.”
Homepage hero
Italics on “you” make it land personally. Outcome-based, not service-based. Short and impossible to misread.
The Rules

Five rules. No exceptions.

Click each rule to expand it. These apply to every piece of content — social posts, website copy, newsletters, ads, and anything you ask AI to write.

1
Make them feel seen. Never say it.
↓

The goal is that a reader feels understood without you claiming you understand them. The moment you say “we get it,” you sound like every other therapy website.

Never write:

You’re not alone We get it We understand We’ve been there You’re not the only one

Write this instead — name the specific feeling in plain language:

“It’s hard to keep up with friends when even getting out of bed feels like work.”

That earns the same emotional beat without announcing it.

2
Stay light, even on heavy topics.
↓

Mental health content can have warmth and even humour without trivialising anything. Doom-laden or serious-faced copy is wrong for Wellspring.

“Adulting is a scam. Most of us were never given the manual.”
“Yes, telling someone with anxiety to just relax has approximately a zero percent success rate.”
3
Describe the feeling, not the diagnosis.
↓

Your patients don’t search for clinical terms. They search for how they feel. Meet them there.

Anxiety and depression treatment
When life feels manageable again
Cognitive behavioural therapy
A way to step out of the spiral
Comprehensive mental health services
Help that meets you where you are
4
Sound like a person, not a practice.
↓

Contractions are fine. Rhetorical questions are good. Short sentences work. You do not need to sound formal to sound professional.

Open with a question Use “you” and “we” Mix short and long sentences
At Wellspring, we provide... “Clients” in headlines
5
Specific beats general, always.
↓

Specifics are what make your content feel real rather than templated.

We help with anxiety about the work week.
Sunday-night dread before a Monday you didn’t sign up for.

Your Audiences

Tone shifts by who you’re speaking to

The voice stays consistent. The register adjusts.

Emerging Adults & Life Transitions

  • Mirror their language. They say “feeling behind,” “stuck,” “can’t adult,” “adulting is a scam.” Use these phrases.
  • Light is essential here. This audience is exhausted by serious-faced advice.
  • This is Wellspring’s biggest intake segment. Every word counts.
“It feels like everyone else figured it out and forgot to send you the memo.”

Teens

  • Speak primarily to the parent, but never patronise the teen.
  • Validate the parent’s helplessness without making them feel guilty.
  • Keep it practical and reassuring.
“You know something is off. You’re not sure how to help. That’s exactly when a conversation with someone outside the family can shift things.”

Women’s Issues

  • Specific, not generic. The mental load. The “everyone needs me” reality.
  • Don’t lecture. Your reader already knows.
  • Practical and grounding beats inspirational.
“There’s a version of fine that isn’t fine at all. It just functions.”

General Adults

  • Default voice — direct, warm, and concrete.
  • Meet them where they are — often mid-decision, not at the beginning.
  • Less handholding, more clarity.
“Most people wait longer than they need to. There’s no wrong time to start — but there’s usually a right one.”

Using AI

How to use this guide with ChatGPT or Claude

Three steps. Works with any AI tool. Takes 30 seconds.

1

Copy the starter prompt below

Core voice rules are already baked in. No need to re-explain them each time.

2

Add your specific request

Tell the AI what format, topic, and audience. Example: “Write an Instagram caption about teen anxiety.”

3

Check against the forbidden list

Scan the output. If a banned phrase appears, ask the AI to rewrite that line. Done.

Starter AI Prompt — copy and paste this
You are writing content for Wellspring Therapeutic Partners, a therapy practice in Troy and Clinton Township, Michigan. Voice rules: - Sound like a thoughtful person who has been through it, not a clinic that knows the textbook - Warm, casual, personal, and light -- even on heavy topics - Describe feelings, not diagnoses ("when life feels manageable again" beats "anxiety treatment") - Specific over generic -- name the concrete feeling, not the category - Never use: "you're not alone," "we get it," "we understand," "reach out today," "take the first step," "wellness journey," "comprehensive mental health services" My request: [write your request here]

The Quick Test

Before anything goes out, read it back like you’re saying it to a friend over coffee.

If you wouldn’t say it that way out loud, rewrite it.

Ask: would Melissa’s or Tabitha’s bio sound like this? If no, rewrite.
